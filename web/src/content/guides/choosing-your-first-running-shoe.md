Walking into a running store (or a website with 400 shoes) for the first time is overwhelming. The good news: most of the marketing is noise. Here are the five things that actually matter.

## 1. Match the shoe to how you run, not the colour

Running shoes fall into a handful of categories, and picking the right **category** matters far more than picking the right brand:

- **Daily trainers** — the do-everything shoe. If you're new, start here.
- **Max cushion** — extra soft and protective for longer, slower miles.
- **Stability** — gentle support if your ankles roll inward (ask a store to check).
- **Tempo and carbon racers** — light and fast, built for race day, not daily use.

A first-timer almost always wants a **daily trainer**. That's why we score every shoe *within its category* — a daily trainer should be compared to other daily trainers, not to a $250 carbon racer.

## 2. Fit beats everything

A shoe that scores a 90 but doesn't fit your foot is a worse shoe for you than an 80 that fits perfectly. Look for:

- A **thumb's width** of space at the front.
- No pinching across the widest part of your foot (check whether the shoe comes in a **wide** option).
- A locked-down heel that doesn't slip.

## 3. Cushioning is a preference, not a quality level

More foam isn't "better" — it's a feel. Some runners love a soft, pillowy ride; others want firm and responsive. Neither is wrong. If you don't know yet, a mid-range daily trainer is a safe default.

## 4. Weight matters less than you think (at first)

A 30-gram difference is meaningful to a competitive racer and irrelevant to someone running three easy miles. Don't pay a premium for "light" until you know you want it.

## 5. Replace them before they're dead

Most shoes are good for **500–800 km**. Past that, the foam stops protecting you and injury risk climbs. Track your mileage loosely and rotate when the ride goes flat.
