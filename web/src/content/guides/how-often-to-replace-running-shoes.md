Running shoes don't announce when they're done. The upper can look almost new while the foam underneath has quietly stopped doing its job — and worn-out foam is one of the most avoidable causes of the niggles that turn into injuries.

## The short answer: 500–800 km

Most modern running shoes are good for **500–800 kilometres** (roughly 300–500 miles). Where your pair falls in that range depends on:

- **The foam.** Soft, bouncy "super foams" (PEBA-based) feel amazing but tend to die younger. Firmer EVA-based trainers usually last longest.
- **Your size and stride.** Heavier runners and heel-strikers compress foam faster.
- **The surface.** Road miles wear outsoles quicker than trail; treadmills are gentlest of all.
- **The shoe's job.** Race-day shoes are deliberately built light at the expense of durability — a carbon racer might only have 200–400 quality kilometres in it. That's the trade you're paying for.

## The real signs your shoes are dead

Kilometres are a guideline; your body and the shoe itself will tell you sooner:

1. **New aches after normal runs.** Shins, knees or arches complaining after runs that used to feel fine is the classic sign the cushioning has gone flat.
2. **The midsole is wrinkled.** Deep creases and compression lines in the foam mean it's compacted and no longer rebounding.
3. **Worn smooth underneath.** If the rubber outsole is worn through to the foam — especially unevenly — the shoe is changing how you land.
4. **They feel flat.** The most honest test: stand in your old pair, then a newer pair. If the old ones feel like planks, believe your feet.

## How to make a pair last longer

- **Rotate two pairs.** Foam rebounds better with a rest day between runs, and two pairs alternated genuinely outlast two pairs run sequentially. Rotation also slightly lowers injury risk by varying the load on your legs.
- **Save the racers for racing.** Do your easy miles in a durable [daily trainer](/categories/daily-trainers/) and save the expensive plated shoe for workouts and race day.
- **Only run in them.** Walking, gym sessions and errands all eat kilometres. Retired running shoes make excellent everyday shoes — that's their second life, not their first.
- **Track your mileage.** Strava and Garmin both let you assign shoes to runs and count the distance for you. Set it up once and you'll never have to guess.

## Don't stockpile, but do plan ahead

When you find a shoe you love, it's tempting to buy three pairs. One spare is sensible (models change every year, not always for the better) — but foam ages slowly even in the box, so a five-year stockpile defeats the purpose.

When it's time, start with the [highest-scoring shoes in your category](/shoes/) — and if your old model was recently updated, check the new version's score before assuming it's the same shoe.
