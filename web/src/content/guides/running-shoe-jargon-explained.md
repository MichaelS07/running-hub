Shoe marketing is a wall of jargon. Here's what the terms on every spec sheet (including ours) actually mean — and honestly, which ones you can ignore.

## Stack height

**What it is:** how much material sits between your foot and the ground, measured in millimetres at the heel and the forefoot.

**What it means for you:** more stack = more cushioning and protection, at some cost of ground feel and, occasionally, stability. Modern shoes have crept taller — 38–42mm heels are now normal, and racing rules cap road shoes at 40mm. Beginners generally do well in the 32–38mm middle ground.

## Drop (or "offset")

**What it is:** the difference between heel stack and forefoot stack. A 36mm heel with a 28mm forefoot is an "8mm drop" shoe.

**What it means for you:** lower drop loads your calves and Achilles a little more; higher drop shifts load toward knees and hips. Most shoes sit between 4–12mm and **most runners adapt to anything in that range** — it matters far less than fit and comfort. The one rule: if you switch to a much lower drop, transition gradually.

One thing our data shows: the drop on the box is often wrong. Lab measurements routinely differ from the advertised number by 2–4mm — it's one reason we list measured values where we have them.

## Carbon plates

**What it is:** a stiff plate (carbon fibre, or sometimes nylon/fibreglass) embedded in the midsole that acts like a lever and stabiliser for the soft foam around it.

**What it means for you:** in a proper racing shoe, a plate plus modern foam is genuinely faster — that's not marketing. But [our own data](/guides/what-a-carbon-plate-actually-gets-you/) shows what you're really buying: lighter weight and more energy return, for considerably more money and less durability. For easy daily miles, you don't need one.

## "Super foam" (PEBA, ZoomX, PWRRUN PB, FF Turbo…)

**What it is:** a family of modern midsole foams that are dramatically lighter and bouncier than classic EVA. Every brand has its own trade name.

**What it means for you:** super foams are why today's shoes can be tall *and* light — a trade-off that [barely exists any more](/guides/cushion-vs-weight-tradeoff/). The catch is durability: the bounciest foams lose their pop sooner. Shoes mixing a super foam top layer with a firmer carrier foam are the sensible middle ground, and it's a very common design now.

## Energy return

**What it is:** the percentage of the energy you put into the foam that you get back as it rebounds, measured in a lab. Ordinary trainers sit around 50–60%; the best racing shoes exceed 70%.

**What it means for you:** it's the closest thing to a measured "bounce" number, and it's a big part of our **Ride** score. But more isn't automatically better for every run — some runners prefer a firmer, quieter ride for easy days.

## Stability, medial posts and guide rails

**What it is:** design features that gently resist your ankle rolling inward (overpronation) — firmer foam wedges, raised sidewalls, or "guide rails."

**What it means for you:** modern stability shoes are subtle, not corrective braces. If your ankles roll in noticeably, or a shop gait check suggests it, start with the [stability category](/categories/stability/). If not, a neutral shoe is fine — extra stability you don't need is just weight.

## The numbers that actually matter

If you remember nothing else: **fit beats every spec on this page**, comfort is a legitimate measurement, and category matters more than brand. The spec sheet helps you choose *between* shoes that fit — start with the [scored database](/shoes/) and go from there.
