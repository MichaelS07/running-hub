Most people who "take up running" quit within a month — not because running is too hard, but because they start too fast, hurt for a week, and decide running isn't for them. Here's the version that sticks.

## Rule one: start slower than feels necessary

The single most common beginner mistake is running every run too fast. Easy runs should feel *conversational* — if you couldn't chat to someone beside you, you're going too hard. Experienced runners do the large majority of their running at this easy effort; beginners tend to do almost none of it. Flip that and everything gets easier.

## The run-walk method (it isn't cheating)

Alternating running and walking is how nearly every successful beginner programme works:

- **Weeks 1–2:** 1 minute running, 2 minutes walking — repeat 8–10 times, three days a week.
- **Weeks 3–4:** 2 minutes running, 1 minute walking — repeat 8 times.
- **Weeks 5–6:** 5 minutes running, 1 minute walking — repeat 4–5 times.
- **Weeks 7–8:** stretch the run blocks until the walks disappear.

Two things matter more than the exact numbers: **never increase by much more than 10% a week**, and **repeat a week if it felt hard**. There is no prize for finishing the plan on schedule; there's a real cost to finishing it injured.

## Rest days are training

Your fitness improves *between* runs, not during them. Three running days a week is plenty for the first couple of months — the days off are when your tendons and joints (which adapt slower than your lungs) catch up. Sore in a "sharp, specific spot" way rather than a "general tiredness" way? Take an extra day. Always.

## You need exactly one piece of gear

A pair of running shoes that fit. Not a watch, not compression socks, not a foam roller — those can all come later if you get hooked. What matters:

- Get a **daily trainer**, the do-everything category — here are [ours, scored and ranked](/categories/daily-trainers/).
- **Fit beats everything**: a thumb's width at the toe, no pinching, heel locked in.
- You don't need to spend big — [our own data shows](/guides/do-expensive-running-shoes-score-better/) price barely predicts quality. Plenty of excellent trainers cost $140 or less.

Old sneakers are fine for week one. If you're still running in week three, buy proper shoes — it's the best money you'll spend on this hobby.

## Give yourself a target: parkrun

Goals beat vague intentions, and the best beginner goal in New Zealand and Australia is **parkrun**: a free, timed 5km every Saturday morning, in a park near you, where walking is completely acceptable and finishers get cheered regardless of pace.

It solves the three things that kill beginner motivation — no route to plan, a fixed time, and other humans. Walk-run your first one, get a finish time, then watch it drop week by week. [Find your local parkrun here](/races/).

## The eight-week version of this whole article

1. Three days a week, run-walk, conversational pace.
2. Increase gently; repeat weeks freely; rest days are sacred.
3. Buy one good pair of [daily trainers](/categories/daily-trainers/) once you're committed.
4. Book a [parkrun](/races/) for week four — walking bits allowed.
5. When 5km feels routine, come back and we'll talk about your first 10K.

That's it. The runners you see gliding along effortlessly all started exactly here.
